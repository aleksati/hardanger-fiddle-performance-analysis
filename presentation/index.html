Performance patterns in Hardanger fiddle music

Hardanger fiddle music is considered vital components of Norwegian folk music traditions, date back to the seventeenth/eighteenth century.

And the name refers to a modiefied version of the original fiddle, with additional sympathetic/resonant stings and wooden ornaments

This music is intimatley related to traditional dances.

Performance patterns in Hardanger fiddle music

  • Springar
    • triplet meter (3/4)
    • Asymmetrical timing patterns.
      • long-average-short, long-long-short, short-long-long etc.

One these dances is springar, a couples dance most often in triplet meter(waltz time signature).

Now, there are many regional and localized "sub-styles" of springar, where some possess the interesting stylistic features of asymmetrical timing patterns.

This refers to the systematic unevenness (asymmetry) in the duration and position of the musical beats.

Given the systamtic unevenness, we often refer to these timing patterns as part of specific durationl categories, such as "long-average-short", "long-long-short" or "short-long-long".

Tool nr.1

Which is in two parts.

Goal

  • Data comprehension
  • Explore alternative performance timings.
  • Address asymmetrical timing patterns from a structural perspective.
  • Increase data accessibility through visualization

Goal is first and foremost data comp, turn the MIRAGE data into something meaningful that users can interacive/engage with, and through that:

enable ability to explore alternative timing patterns and rhythmic strucuring in the UI.

Part 1

Interactive Score

Graphical score representation

Its a mix of piano roll and score representation, with time in seconds on the X-axis, and green bars represent musical beats.

As you can see, the user has the ability to dynamically adjust the beat positions as they wish. And listen.

Thats not the only thing, we can also (for instance) choose not to temporally companste for our adjustments and rather shift the whole score.

This way, our adjustments dont affect the neightborign beats. A longer beat = a longer score.

Also...

implemented the ability to impose average beat timing to ALL measures in the performance.

Inspired by more convential research into asymmetrical timing patterns. which sought more to generalize/average the asymmetrical performance timing.

The 3 numbers under quantize refer to the 3 beats in our time signature.

If we select 40,40,20, EVERY first and second beat make up 40% each of their associated measure. and EVERY 3rd beat 20%

Export various versions and compare.

Part 2

Timing patterns of repeating sections and phrases

The second tool explores timing patterns of repeating sections or phrases. 2nd design pillar.

Breif recap, this feature/tool takes advantage of the structural analysis mentioned done by Olivier my supervisor.

It does this by visually represeting the relationship between repeating motifs/sections and timing patterns.

I will get into the details of this now

So here is the first prototype in full, with tool nr.1 and nr.2

first thing to notice is the dictionary. Converts p data into more firendly and high-level format. which we can export.

second, in addition to perf-data, we can add the structural data discussed in the previous slide. which adds the data to the dict.

What we now can do is:

Choose a range (from a bar to a bar), click plot, and the program will see if the particular range we selected repeats itself in the peformance (is part of a phrase, motif or section). If it finds any, it colors the sections and plots the timing patterns of all those repetitions.

The Plot shows beats along the X-axis and Beat durations along the Y-axis.

In this example, we found 4 instances of a repeating phrase (from b 17-18). As we can see, the timing patterns of these repititions are fairly consistent throughout the performance, only with greater variations at the start.

Additionally, various plotting settings enable us to view the data in various ways.

Comments

  • Note scaling when adjusting beats.
  • Propagate beat adjustments to all repetition instances.

    • Important to note that this tool is still under development, so features are constantly added and improved.

    An example is note scaling when adjusting the beats (part 1). Research suggests that there often are some notes that are more essential to the beat length than others.

    Similarly, having the ability to propegate the adjustments you make to one section to all of its repeating instances, would also be interesting to explore

    This last feature I have actually developed, but not yet implemented because of time and priorirtes.

Tool nr.2

Musical properties of repeating timing patterns

Goal

  • Enable multi-dimensional analysis
    • Examining property relationships between regions that share similar timing patterns.
      • Pitch
      • Velocity
      • Beat/motivic position
        •

Select a region with mouse.

The program then interpretes the note durations of the selection as a "timing pattern".

Another way to view this is through a graph where the note amount is on X, and their durations in on Y.

Imagine our previous note selection is whats inside this circle.

The program understands this as a pattern and tries to find similar patterns in the performance, basically.

If it does find any patterns, it selects them in our score. So in this picture I have selected two random regions as an example.

Then we can extract and compare properties from those regions.

Choose which properties we want to examine, select a note region and hit the button. If it finds any patterns, it colors and plots them.

So in this example, its found 2 region that share similar timing pattern, and we compare their metrical position.

We can also compare pitch content.

Lastly I want to draw your attention to this scale slider.

Since its hard to find exact pattern matches (if not impossible), this slider ROUNDS all the timing values to higher and higher numbers.

Making pattern finding much easier.

The higher the scale factor, the more regions we will find that have similar timing patterns.

Comments

  • Timing pattern formalization (IOI)
  • Motivic position

Timing pattern formalization is not optimal at all, but serves this prototyping purpose well. We would probably much rather define timing patterns in this context as a collection of inter-onset intervals or something along those lines.

Finally, a very nice feature would be to examine the motivic or structural position of the regions with similar patterns. This is highly possible, but is only limited by the scope of this thesis.
